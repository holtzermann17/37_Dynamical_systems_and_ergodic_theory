Sharkovskii’s theorem


Every natural number can be written as 2r⁢p, where p is odd, and r is the maximum exponent such that 2r divides (http://planetmath.org/Divisibility) the given number. We define the Sharkovskii ordering of the natural numbers in this way: given two odd numbers p and q, and two nonnegative integers r and s, then 2r⁢p≻2s⁢q if

  1. 1.

    r<s and p>1;

  2. 2.

    r=s and p<q;

  3. 3.

    r>s and p=q=1.

This defines a linear ordering of ℕ, in which we first have 3,5,7,…, followed by 2⋅3, 2⋅5,…, followed by 22⋅3, 22⋅5,…, and so on, and finally 2n+1,2n,…,2,1. So it looks like this:

3≻5≻⋯≻3⋅2≻5⋅2≻⋯≻3⋅2n≻5⋅2n≻⋯≻22≻2≻1.

Sharkovskii’s theorem. Let I⊂ℝ be an interval, and let f:I→ℝ be a continuous function. If f has a periodic point of least period n, then f has a periodic point of least period k, for each k such that n≻k.
